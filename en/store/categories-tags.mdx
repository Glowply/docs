**Categories (التصنيفات)** and **Tags (الوسوم)** are the two ways you organize the products in your **Store**. They turn a long list of items into something customers can browse and filter — so shoppers find what they want faster and buy more.

You'll find both in your **site dashboard** under **Store (المتجر)**: **Categories (التصنيفات)** and **Tags (الوسوم)** are separate sections in the side menu. This is different from your **account portal**, which is only for billing.

<Info>
  Not sure how to open your site dashboard? See [Site dashboard overview](/en/site/dashboard-overview). Categories and tags live in the **site dashboard**, not the account portal.
</Info>

## Categories vs. tags

Both group your products, but they work differently. Most stores use **a few broad categories** for their main sections and **tags** for keywords that cut across those sections.

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Categories" icon="folder-tree">
    The **main groups** that structure your Store, like *Dresses*, *Shoes*, or *Accessories*. They're usually shown as a browse menu, and a category can have **subcategories** (such as *Dresses → Summer dresses*). Think of them as the aisles of your shop.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Tags" icon="tags">
    **Free-form keywords** that label related products across different categories — like *sale*, *handmade*, *gift*, or *summer*. Tags are flat (no parent or child) and a product can have many. Think of them as sticky labels.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

Here's a quick way to tell them apart:

| | Categories | Tags |
|---|---|---|
| **Purpose** | Main structure of your Store | Cross-cutting keywords |
| **Hierarchy** | Can have subcategories | Flat — no levels |
| **How many per product** | Usually one or a few | As many as make sense |
| **Where customers see them** | Browse menu and product pages | Product pages and tag links |
| **Example** | *Dresses*, *Shoes* | *sale*, *new*, *gift* |

<Note>
  You don't have to choose one or the other — you use both together. A *Summer Dress* might sit in the **Dresses** category and also carry the tags **summer** and **sale**.
</Note>

## Create a category

Categories are the backbone of your Store, so it helps to plan a few main groups before you add lots of products.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Open Categories">
    In your site dashboard, go to **Store → Categories**. You'll see any categories you already have, along with how many products are in each.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Add a new category">
    Choose to **add a new category** and give it a clear **name** — the word customers will see, such as *Candles* or *Accessories*. Keep it short and obvious.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Set a parent (optional)">
    To make this a **subcategory**, pick a **parent category**. For example, set *Summer dresses* under the parent *Dresses*. Leave the parent empty to create a top-level category.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Add a description and image (optional)">
    Add a short **description** and a **category image** if you'd like. Some store layouts show these on the category's browse page, which makes your Store look more polished.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Save the category">
    Save it. The new category appears in your list right away and is ready for products.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Tip>
  Start with **3–7 broad categories** that match how customers think — *Tops*, *Bottoms*, *Accessories* — rather than dozens of tiny ones. You can always add subcategories later as your catalog grows.
</Tip>

### Build subcategories

A **subcategory** is just a category with a parent. Use them to break a large category into smaller, browsable groups:

- *Clothing → Dresses, Tops, Trousers*
- *Accessories → Bags, Jewelry, Hats*

Subcategories show up under their parent in the browse menu, so customers can drill down from a broad section to exactly what they want. Keep the structure shallow — one or two levels is plenty for most stores.

<Warning>
  Avoid deep chains of subcategories (category inside category inside category). They make your Store harder to navigate. If you're going more than two levels deep, a **tag** is often the better tool.
</Warning>

## Assign products to categories

A product can belong to one category or several. There are two easy ways to set this.

<Steps>
  <Step title="From the product (one at a time)">
    Open a product in **Store → Products**, find its **Categories** section, and tick the categories it belongs to. You can also **add a new category** right there without leaving the product. Save the product when you're done. See [Products](/en/store/products).
  </Step>
  <Step title="In bulk (several at once)">
    In the **Products** list, select the products you want, then use the **bulk edit** action to add them to a category together. This is the fastest way to organize a catalog you've already built.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Note>
  If you don't assign a product to any category, your Store may place it in a default "uncategorized" group. Give every product at least one real category so it shows up where customers expect.
</Note>

## Create and use tags

Tags are quicker than categories — there's no hierarchy to plan. Add them as keywords whenever they're useful.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Add tags from the product">
    Open a product and find its **Tags** section. Type a keyword such as *gift* or *handmade* and add it. Reuse the same spelling each time so the tag stays consistent (for example, always *sale*, not *Sale* one time and *on-sale* the next).
  </Step>
  <Step title="Manage tags in one place">
    Go to **Store → Tags** to see every tag, how many products use each, rename a tag, or delete ones you no longer need. Cleaning up duplicates here keeps your filters tidy.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Tip>
  Tags work best for things that cross categories — a seasonal **summer** promotion, a **bestseller** badge, or a **gift** collection that pulls items from several categories into one place.
</Tip>

## How customers use them

On your live Store, categories and tags become the tools shoppers use to find things:

- **Categories** appear as a **browse menu** and on each product page, so customers can move from a broad section into its subcategories.
- **Tags** link related products together — clicking a tag like **gift** shows every product you've labeled that way, even if they're in different categories.
- Both can power **filters**, letting customers narrow a page down to exactly what they're after.

<Note>
  Good organization is one of the easiest ways to sell more. When customers can browse and filter naturally, they find more of what they want — and discover products they weren't looking for.
</Note>

## Tips for organizing your Store

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Should I use a category or a tag?">
    Use a **category** for a main section of your Store that customers would browse to — like *Shoes*. Use a **tag** for a keyword that spans sections — like *sale* or *gift*, which could apply to shoes, bags, or anything else. A simple rule: categories are *where* a product lives; tags are *what it's like*.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="How many categories should I have?">
    Start small — around **3 to 7 broad categories** that match how your customers think. Add subcategories only when a category grows large enough that customers need help narrowing it down. Too many categories are as confusing as too few.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Can a product be in more than one category?">
    Yes. A product can belong to several categories and carry several tags at once. For example, a striped summer dress could sit in **Dresses** and **New arrivals**, with the tags **summer** and **sale**.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="What happens if I delete a category or tag?">
    Deleting a category or tag removes that grouping — it does **not** delete the products in it. The products stay in your Store; they simply lose that label. If a product ends up with no category, your Store may show it as "uncategorized" until you assign a new one.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="My tags are a mess — how do I clean them up?">
    Open **Store → Tags** and look for near-duplicates (*sale*, *on sale*, *sales*). Pick one spelling, reassign products to it, and delete the rest. Keeping a short, consistent tag list makes your filters far more useful to customers.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="What's the difference from product attributes?">
    Categories and tags help customers **find and browse** products. **Attributes** (like size and color) define the **options** within a single product and power its variations at checkout. They solve different problems — see [Attributes](/en/store/attributes).
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
